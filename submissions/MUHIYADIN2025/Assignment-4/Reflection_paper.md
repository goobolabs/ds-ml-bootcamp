Reflection Paper: Car Price Prediction Analysis1. 
Project Implementation & Model TrainingIn this practical assignment, I implemented a machine learning workflow to predict used car prices using the preprocessed dataset from Assignment Three. The training pipeline evaluated two distinct algorithms:Linear Regression: Trained as a baseline parametric model to map linear relationships between car characteristics (e.g., mileage, engine size, year) and the target market price.Random Forest Regressor: Implemented as a non-parametric tree-based ensemble model to capture complex, non-linear patterns and multi-feature interactions without relying on strict data distribution assumptions.Both models were trained on an 80/20 train-test split, utilizing scaled features derived from the previous cleaning process.2. Comparison of Models & Sanity CheckDuring the native sanity check—where specific test cases (e.g., a highly driven car versus a low-mileage luxury car) were fed into both models—the predictions diverged significantly:
Linear Regression Predictions: Occasionally generated less realistic outcomes, sometimes predicting near-zero or overly suppressed prices for older vehicles with high mileage due to its rigid straight-line extrapolation.Random Forest Predictions: Provided significantly more realistic and bounded pricing scales.Why Random Forest performed better:Random Forest logic aligns perfectly with real-world automobile deprecation. Car prices do not drop infinitely in a straight line; they hit a baseline value regardless of age. Random Forest naturally captures these step-like thresholds, whereas Linear Regression forces a constant rate of decline across all feature spectrums.3. Understanding Random ForestIn my own words, Random Forest is an ensemble learning method that builds a "forest" of multiple individual Decision Trees during the training phase. Instead of relying on a single tree—which is highly prone to memorizing training noise—Random Forest introduces randomness by training each tree on a random subset of data points (bootstrapping) and a random selection of features.When a new car profile is passed into the model, every individual decision tree outputs its own independent price prediction. The Random Forest algorithm then aggregates these diverse outputs and calculates the final price by averaging the predictions of all the trees. This collective vote dramatically reduces variance and protects the model against overfitting.4. Metrics Discussion & EvaluationBased on the performance metrics computed on the unseen testing partition:Better Performer: The Random Forest Regressor achieved a significantly higher $R^2$ score alongside much lower MAE (Mean Absolute Error) and RMSE (Root Mean Squared Error) metrics compared to Linear Regression.Insights into Strengths & Weaknesses:Linear Regression: Its strength lies in absolute simplicity and speed, making it highly interpretable. However, its core weakness is high bias (underfitting); it fails to grasp non-linear correlations, such as how brand prestige or regional popularity disproportionately shifts a vehicle's value.
Random Forest: Its strength is its exceptional predictive accuracy and capacity to handle mixed data types and non-linear interactions natively. Its primary weakness is that it functions as a "black box," making it harder to explain the exact mathematical weight of a single feature, and it cannot extrapolate values outside its training boundaries.5. Final Findings & Model PreferenceBased on the empirical evidence gathered throughout this project, I firmly prefer the Random Forest Regressor for used car price prediction pipelines. Automobile valuation is inherently multi-dimensional; a vehicle's price degradation curve is heavily influenced by non-linear thresholds, such as psychological mileage benchmarks (e.g., crossing 100,000 km) and categorical interactions like trim packages and brand categories. Random Forest manages these distinct variances with minimal manual tuning, preventing the massive error skewing that outliers often cause in simpler setups.While Linear Regression remains an excellent diagnostic asset for baseline verification, it lacks the operational depth required for reliable deployment in commercial pricing engines. Random Forest delivers stable, tightly bounded, and highly accurate price estimates, making it the superior architecture for modern vehicle valuation systems.
